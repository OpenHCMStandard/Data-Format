OpenHCM Description

 

1     Summary

 

One of the main objectives of the CAS Project is to create an open data exchange standard that can be used widely by all stakeholders involved in the Process "Hull Condition Monitoring and Assessment" (HCMA), such as Classification Societies, Measurement Companies, Ship and Cargo Owners (e.g. Oil Majors) and Maritime Authorities (Flag States, IMO, EMSA, National Coast Guards, etc.).

This document contains the specification of an industry specific XML-vocabulary called OpenHCM (Open Hull Condition Model). OpenHCM is an open Data Model for structuring information concerning the condition of the ship’s hull structure, the focus within the CAS project was on thickness measurements. The model can be extended to other survey findings (e.g. cracks, deformations, coating condition, etc.) on ship structural elements.

A major design goal of the OpenHCM is that it shall not be necessary to enter more information in preparation and execution of measurements than today. The information content of an OpenHCM file can therefore reflect the amount of information contained today in a typical thickness measurement report. On the other hand, a 3D geometrical model of a ship can simplify assessment of measurement results when used as a means of visualization. Such a model can be stored in OpenHCM format, but again in a very simple form. In contrast to a full structural model of the ship, a data model for HCMA needs no topological information (connectivity of plates) and only low precision with respect to the exact location of plate boundaries. OpenHCM is therefore held simple as compared to structural models such as STEP-AP218: its complexity is tailored to the HCMA process.

OpenHCM shall provide support for all phases of HCMA viz. Planning, Recording, Reporting and Assessment. The chosen XML-based approach ensures extensibility in future versions of the data model, which will need to be adapted to new available methodology for ship inspections (e.g. robotics), new regulatory requirements (IMO, EMSA, IACS) as well as to innovations in Information Technology in both soft- and hardware that will have an impact on the process. In spite of possible extensions, it shall always be possible to use OpenHCM in a minimal way, i.e. by storing only that information about the ship structure and the gaugings, which can be captured during today's typical measurement campaign.

OpenHCM is implemented in form of a set of Schema Documents based on the W3C XML Schema Specification [1], [2], [3].

2     Introduction (non normative)

The Open Hull Condition Data Model (OpenHCM) enables capturing all information required for determining and analysing the corrosion status of a vessel as required in current mandatory regulations[1]. It includes constructs for simple geometry information of plates and profiles. This simple geometry information enables the graphical representation of the structures for both data collection and visualisation of the measurements and their associations with plates and profiles of a full structural model as needed, e.g. for strength analysis. OpenHCM also provides support for the use of "incomplete models" as well as for the incremental definition/population of the model data.

2.1 Simplified Geometry vs. Complex 3D Model

The basis for data collection for Hull Condition Asessement is the knowledge about the structure of the ship. This knowledge is used for:

a)      specifying thickness measurement locations,

b)      locating plates and profiles during the measurement or survey and

c)      associating measured thicknesses (and other findings) with plates and profiles in the report and assessment.

This structural information is today contained in the steel drawings. In the event of a thickness measurement campaign, the company contracted to carry out the work obtains the steel drawings from the owner and prepares adequate versions of the drawings for the measurements. In case no steel drawings are available (this might be the case with old ships, which have changed the owner several times) sketches of the measured structural members are elaborated from scratch. This is typically done by the Thickness Measurements (TM) firm.

Today, the measured thicknesses together with sketches are sufficient information for the later steps in the assessment. Therefore, the fundamental question in the design of a data model for HCMA is: “how can this information be organised so that:

·         not more information than today needs to be entered,

·         data acquired once, needs to be entered only once, and

·         maximal computer support for the assessment phase (visualization and longitudinal strength) could be given?”

In TM sketches, only the information which is relevant for identifying the plates and profiles in the real ship is required (welding seams defining the shape and position of individual plates, original thickness of the plates, type and position of profiles), see e.g. Figure 2‑1.

In contrast to an analysis model of the ship, a data model for HCMA needs no topological information (i.e. connectivity of plates) and only low precision with respect to the exact location of plate boundaries is required. In particular, gaps or overlaps between adjacent plates in the model pose no problem, as long as it is possible for the TM staff to identify the particular plate in the real ship. In this way, only a simplified 3D model needs to be built up and stored. Its complexity must be tailored to the measurement process.

Figure 2‑1: Shell expansion sketch for TM purposes.

At present, the tabular representation of the data is typically prepared in the office after the measurement campaign, while preparing the TM report. Depending on the size of the vessel and the scope of the measurements, this procedure may take up to several weeks. At this stage the matching between the sketch and the table is established. This is usually realised by numbering the measurements and using numbers/names or any other kind of denominations for plates and profiles.

When analysing the thickness measurements, the scantling requirements of the classification rules (corrosion diminutions) are checked for the individual plates and profiles. In the future, this task will be supported by a visualisation of the corroded state of the vessel.

For oil tankers and in some cases for other ship types (approx. 5% according to the experience of Germanischer Lloyd) the analysis is extended to longitudinal strength calculations using the measured hull condition, see Figure 2‑2. For this purpose, the current thickness of plates and profiles must be input into a calculation system (such as POSEIDON or VeriSTAR-Hull), which is usually based on a more complex 3D representation of the steel structure. Today the measurements have to be entered manually into such a program and an engineer might require up to several weeks to achieve this task. Too long, considering the relatively short time the vessel is in the dock.

Although current TM reports might be available electronically (e.g. as Excel sheets), there is no functional support for the analysis. An additional problem comes from the fact that different naming conventions for plates and profiles may be used in these tables for the same vessel depending on the company in charge of the measurements.

It is expected, that significant improvements can be achieved for the HCMA process using a simplified electronic model of the ship and the measurements as described above. In particular, faster assessment through visualisation and longitudinal strength calculation will become possible.

Figure 2‑2: Simplified Geometry vs. complex 3D model in the TM process

Nevertheless, the key value of OpenHCM is the flexibility to represent the different aspects of the data involved in various stages of the process as well as the definition of a formal and well described interface to support different type of information like:

·         general data about the ship and the measurement campaign,

·         measurements data,

·         simplified geometrical information of the measured/surveyed structural components

·         identification of replacements of plates or profiles,

·         Reference to survey report items (to be done),

OpenHCM enables the storage of thickness measurement data without an existing 3D-model of the ship. The measurement points will then be associated with an explicit location on the ship using absolute positions and additional attributes to facilitate the assignment to the respective structural component.

2.2 Top level structure of OpenHCM

OpenHCM is divided in the following modules.

·         hcm-pdt - Generic Product Data: It defines data constructs for common used information about a product during its life cycle. This concerns general product data, which is not shipbuilding specific.

·         hcm-sdt - Generic Ship Data: It defines the shipbuilding specific data constructs for describing the structural elements (plates and profiles), including a simplified geometry representation, frame spacing tables and other general vessel information of interest (e.g. main dimensions, class information, etc.).

·         hcm-core - Thickness Measurements Data: It defines the data constructs for representing all the data collected during a measurement campaign. It includes constructs for different kinds of measurements (plate strake based measurements, cross section based measurements, measurements on profiles, etc.) and their relationship to the corresponding structural elements. Data about the measurement campaign (date / company / …) is also included.

·         hcm-model- OpenHCM Model: It defines the structure of the Hull Condition Model containing the information sets required to transport the condition of the vessel after being determined by survey, measurement  or repair activity (campaign).

·         hcm: The top level schema which used for the purpose of defining a namespace.

Figure 2‑3 shows the different modules of OpenHCM and their relationships expressed as an UML Package Diagram.

All tools being developed during the CAS project (interfaces to Classification Software, geometry and measurement input modules, visualisation module, condition assessment and risk based analysis tools) were based on OpenHCM.

3     Technology Background

For the implementation of the data model, the “Extensible Mark-up Language” (XML) has been chosen as the basic technology. The Extensible Mark-up Language (XML) represents a wide-spread technology for structuring documents and any kind of data that needs to be transmitted, stored or presented to users. An XML Schema approach is used to define the structure of OpenHCM. As a modern and robust approach for structuring data, XML provides mechanisms for creating, storing and exchanging complex organised data by and between software applications and systems with or without human interaction. XML enables the combination of data contents and meaning of the data through its "mark-up" approach. At the same time it supports the strict separation of data and its presentation, which plays an important role in the design and development of technical software, making possible to provide different views from the same source data.

Being derived from SGML (Standard Generalized Mark-up Language, ISO 8879), XML was initially designed and used for structuring electronic documents for the web. However, due to its extensibility and wide availability it rapidly became the favorite technology of software developers for defining industry specific data formats, so called "XML vocabularies". In fact, XML is not only connected to the XML specification 1.0 published by the World Wide Web consortium (W3C), Bray T. el al. , it is to be understood as a family of related and mutually complementing technologies, which extend the utilisation of XML to many business areas, including the storage, exchange and manipulation of complex technical data.

3.1 Document-Centric vs. Data-Centric

XML technology is being implemented and used for a wide variety of purposes in different domains of business and industry. Depending on the context for which XML is used, XML formatted documents can be categorized into two major groups: Document-centric and Data-centric.

3.2 XML Schema

The structure of the Hull Condition Model is defined in the XML Schema Definition Language (XSDL) as specified by the World Wide Web Consortium. XSDL was chosen due to the advantages that it provides in comparison to Document Type Definitions.

4     Scope

The purpose of OpenHCM is to provide data exchange support in all phases of the HCMA process.

Following aspects are within the scope of OpenHCM:

·         General information about the measured ship

·         Information about measured structural elements

·         General information about the measurement campaign

·         Information about the individual plates and profiles

·         Simplified geometry description of plates and profiles

·         Information about the individual measurements (position, value, plate)

·         References to external files such as photographs, documents, and spoken comments

 

Following aspects are outside the scope of OpenHCM and must be provided by the corresponding application using the data contained in a OpenHCM instance file

·         Results of Data Analysis

·         Data used for the visual representation of the measurements (attributes like color and line thickness, symbols, etc.)

 

 

 

 

 

 

5     Architecture of OpenHCM

The OpenHCM has been developed in five steps

1.      identification of the requirements through a process analysis,

2.      process modelling with focus on the data exchange between sub-processes,

3.      a textual data description,

4.      design of the data model, and

5.      refinement of the data model during software implementation and testing/validation.

Steps (1) to (3) are documented in deliverable D-1-2-1 "Report on Business Process Analysis and User Requirements" [13]. The results of step (4) are described in the present document.

HCM is based on the W3C XML Schema Definition Language (WSDL). However, since the the Object Oriented Paradigm is adopted, a preliminary data modelling is implemented by means of the graphical approach provided by Class Diagrams of the Unified Modelling Language (UML) [7].

5.1 XML Schema Design

Following the "venetian blind" XML Schema design pattern, Costello R.[8] and McBeath D. et al [10], most OpenHCM modules contain, at the global schema document level, only definitions of element types. In the whole data model, only one global element definition is contained, avoiding ambiguity for the root element and supporting extensibility.

Figure 5‑1 on next page shows a graphical representation of OpenHCM as simple Unified Modelling Language (UML) class diagram, showing the relationships between different data constructs.

The following aspects of XML Schema design have been taken into consideration:

Modularisation: The Data model is built up of several XML Schema Documents, which are interconnected by means of the include and import features of WSDL.

Design Patterns: the Venetian Blind design pattern [8] is followed in order to achieve best extensibility of the data model, i.e. all globally declared data constructs are defined as simple or complex types. Only the data construct, which will become the root element in the instance documents, is declared globally as an element. In fact, most of the implemented schema documents cannot be instantiated, since they contain only data types to be used by other modules.

Extensibility:

The chosen XML Schema approach for data modelling provides adequate mechanisms to make the model extensible.

Development Guidelines:

To ensure that deployment of OpenHCM by software developers does not lead to a misuse of technical possibilities provided by the XML schema technology, a set of guidelines for XML developers have been formulated. Important aspects like the extension of OpenHCM with own data constructs are covered by these Guidelines.

5.2 Design Objectives

The main objective of OpenHCM is to provide support for the HCMA process. One of the major aspects in the development of the data model – due to the huge amount of data being currently processed by hand – is the support for thickness measurement procedures as required by international regulations and applicable rules of classification societies. Following the approach described in [9]  for the identification of design objectives of XML Schemas, OpenHCM has been designed to be:

1.      Simple: It supports the current procedure of thickness measurements, i.e. the information in a measurement file (instance document) is similar to the content (not to the format) of IACS tables combined with sketches as used today.

2.      Flexible: It is possible to use the data model even if no structural model (e.g. in classification software) is available beforehand.

3.      Descriptive: It enables easy visualization of the measurement results.

4.      Unambiguous: In case a structural model exists, matching of thickness measurements to elements in the structural model is supported.

5.      Independent: measurement files can be processed by different applications implemented by different parties.

6.      Compatible: Compatibility with current working procedures is provided. In particular, generation of IACS tables for reporting TM is possible. Nevertheless, the structure of the data model is independent of the organisation of the data in the IACS tables.

7.      Self-contained: One file contains all data from one measurement campaign. The data model does not depend on external XML schemas.

8.      Compact: Measurement files are reasonably small (e.g. by avoiding long names for elements and attributes).

9.      Extensible: Software developers are empowered to extend the data model as required for specific purposes.

10.  Open: OpenHCM (not the measurement data, but the data model) is freely available to all parties interested in developing software supporting Hull Condition Monitoring and Assessment (e.g. Classification Societies or software vendors). No proprietary data of any party's software system is included in the data model.

 

 

6     OpenHCM Specification (normative)

6.1 General Considerations

The OpenHCM specification is based on XML Schema Definition Language (XSDL) [1], [2], [3] and [4]. The following design criteria have been used throughout all schema documents.

6.1.1        Naming conventions

For names of Complex Types and Elements the Upper Camel Case (UCC) rule is used, i.e. only the first letter of the name is uppercase and in composed names every individual name starts with upper case in one single word; for instance: ShipData

For names of Attributes and Simple Types, the Lower Camel Case (LCC) rule is used; i.e. similar to the UCC rule, but the first letter is in lower case.

Word separation by means of any other symbol (dash "-", underline "_", dot ".", etc.) is prohibited.   

Use of plural form is reserved for constructs denoting a group or set of elements, for instance: ThicknessMeasurements

Abbreviations should not be used. However, widely accepted acronyms may be used as part of composed names. Acronyms should be always in upper case. The UCC and LCC conventions mentioned above for element and type names and for attribute names respectively must be observed.

6.1.2        Namespace

The OpenHCM namespace is temporarily defined by http://www.hcm.org/XML/1.0/hcm. The name space URI (unified resource identifier, [11]) may be changed once OpenHCM is accepted as an IACS standard. Version handling is thus supported in the namespace and 1.0 is the first official version.

The individual schema documents do not define a default namespace. However, the schema document hcm.xsd is used to define the above mentioned URI as default namespace of the data model.

6.1.3        Physical Units

All quantities are stored in SI units (m, kg, s, K or combinations thereof).

6.1.4        Global Ship Coordinate System

The origin of the global ship coordinate system is situated at frame 0 (rudder axis), centreline and basis line respectively. The positive x-axis goes in forward direction, the positive y-axis goes on the port side and the positive z-axis goes upwards. In Figure 6‑1 the global coordinate is shown.
